                                                     A BSTRACT
        This paper studies a deliberately early question: how can distinguishability begin before geometry,
        locality, carrier structure, calculus, or later physical machinery have been introduced? The answer
        developed here is a root formalism in which isolated nullity and isolated undifferentiated totality
        are not viable realized origins for a law-bearing branch. The admissible origin is instead a single
        primitive condition carrying unresolved pole-opposition internally. From that condition the paper
        derives non-flat distinguishability, the first admitted axis of articulation, same-axis saturation, and
        the forced move into a second irreducible axis. The result is a compact 0D–1D–2D formal spine for
        CF000: invariant-bearing articulation appears first, continuous same-axis bifurcation supplies the
        1D effective invariant, and the second axis supplies the first numerical relation-grid in which algebra
        becomes meaningful. The paper is intended to be read as a constructive starting point for the later
        carrier and geometric stages, not as a finished theory of those later stages.

Keywords primitive origin, distinguishability, invariant-bearing articulation, orthogonal re-articulation, same-axis
saturation, algebra unlock

Reader Orientation
This paper begins at the pre-carrier layer of the present VDM branch. Its purpose is to make the first step of
distinguishability explicit before later structures such as geometry, locality, differentiability, QGT, or metriplectic
dynamics are brought into the story.
The central object is one primitive origin-condition. It is not a point in a background space and it is not a pair of already
separated things. It is a single admissible condition in which the two terminal poles, absolute nullity and absolute
undifferentiated totality, remain unresolved while being borne internally. The constructive question is then simple: once
that unresolved burden is admitted, what must be carried next?
The answer developed below is the 0D–1D–2D spine. The origin first yields non-flat articulation; the first admitted
axis carries continuous same-axis bifurcation; saturation of that axis forces a second irreducible axis; and only at that
two-axis stage does the first numerical relation-grid, and therefore algebraic description, become available.

Contribution and Scope
The paper contributes a self-contained root formalism for the earliest admissible route from primitive unresolved origin
to two-axis articulation. The main contribution is not a numerical simulation and not a later physical model. It is a
dependency-clean derivation of the first articulation structure used by the downstream CF stack.
The work establishes:

1. the exclusion of isolated nullity and isolated undifferentiated totality as realized branch roots,
2. the forced admissible origin as one condition bearing unresolved primitive opposition,
3. the first non-flat invariant-bearing articulation,
4. same-axis saturation and forced orthogonal re-articulation,
5. 0D–1D–2D closure,
6. the 1D effective invariant of continuous same-axis bifurcation,
7. the 2D numerical relation-grid invariant and the first algebraic unlock.

Later structures are kept in their proper place. Carrier construction, QGT, metric curvature, Laplacian structure,
differentiability, and the named J/M machinery belong downstream. They are valuable continuations of the program,
but they are not used as primitive inputs here.

Companion Materials
A companion notebook is included to make the paper easier to explore computationally. The notebook does not
replace the derivation. It gives small executable witnesses for the paper’s main structural claims: primitive candidate
comparison, the 0D–1D–2D stage map, same-axis saturation, and the first two axis-proper effective invariants.
The release also includes a Lean 4 surface file and a SymPy script for the finite witness layer. Their role is modest
and useful: they make the primitive-survivor count, saturation index, and relation-grid counts easy to inspect as exact
Boolean and integer claims.
The notebook is written as a readable exploration. Its role is to help a reader see the formal spine move in a concrete
finite witness model, collect the key measurements, and provide a reproducible starting point for later CFN work.

How to Read the Formalism
The manuscript uses a strict dependency order. Definitions appear before the theorems that use them; later machinery is
mentioned only as downstream context; and empirical consistency notes are separated from theorem-bearing claims.
Status labels such as “Proved in this CF,” “Derived structural consequence,” and “Forward note” are used to help the
reader know what kind of claim is being made. They are not meant to turn the paper into a compliance report. They are
a compact guide to where the mathematical burden lives and where later work naturally continues.
The short version is: Sections 1–4 carry the root derivation, Sections 5–7 show consequences and minimal witnesses,
Section 8 explains the notebook pairing, and Sections 9–11 collect scope, integration, provenance, and reusable notation.

1. Scope, Ontology, and Primitive Commitments
1.1 Root question

CF00 begins later than the absolute root. CF000 exists because that is already too late for an origin account. The
root question of this document is: What is the earliest admissible primitive law from which distinguishability, higher
articulation, and the first lawful orthogonal re-articulation can be earned without primitive geometry, carrier structure,
locality, time, causality, valuation, parameterization, or differentiability?

1.2 Declared closure and stage boundary

This CF closes only the staged route:
0D -> 1D -> 2D. Here that means:

      • 0D: primitive unresolved origin before admitted axis-specific articulation,
      • 1D: first admitted axis of non-flat invariant-bearing articulation,
      • 2D: the first orthogonal re-articulation forced when the 1D axis is exhausted without discharge. At each
        admitted axis/domain within this closure, the invariant is not merely preserved but re-expressed as a new
        axis-proper effective invariant proper to that admission: first at 1D as continuous same-axis bifurcation
        and again at 2D as the numerical relation-grid invariant. This CF does not prove QGT, metric geometry,
        carrier-family mechanics, differentiability, or the named conservative/dissipative split. Those belong later.

1.3 Primitive ontology

The primitive vocabulary is deliberately small. CF000 starts with the terminal pole marks, their non-identity, their
sterility for realized differentiated articulation, their opposition, an origin-candidate predicate, a non-vacuousness
predicate, a realizability predicate, a flatness predicate, and the one-origin invariant discipline.
From that compact vocabulary, the paper derives the following structures: primitive invariant carriage, differentiated
articulation, the lowest-resolution invariant-bearing degree, genuinely higher-resolution degree, recursive restatement,
contextual noncoincidence, apartness, pre-metric orthogonal re-articulation, same-axis saturation, the 1D axis-proper
effective invariant, continuous same-axis bifurcation, serial/proto-natural-number structure at 1D, inherited invariant
stacking at 2D, the 2D numerical relation-grid invariant, 2D closure, and algebra unlock.

1.4 Stage boundaries and deferred structure

The following structures are intentionally deferred at the CF000 layer. They are not rejected; they are simply not
primitive resources for this paper’s root derivation:

      • carrier language,
      • topology, metric structure, or chart language,
      • locality or neighborhood structure,
      • time parameter, event sequence, propagation, or causality,
      • graph, node, edge, or tree language,
      • QGT,
      • named J, M, or J ⊕ M machinery,
      • differentiability, Laplacian, or smooth support structure.

Keeping these objects downstream is what lets CF000 state its own burden cleanly: the first articulation has to be earned
before the later carrier and geometric vocabulary can be used.

Let C denote the logical domain of candidate realization conditions. This is not a primitive ontology of objects in a
world. It is only a logical range of discourse, introduced so that realizability claims can be stated without presupposing
points, carriers, graphs, or manifolds.

0 ̸= 1

2.4 One-origin and no-prior-apartness discipline

The manuscript adopts the following root discipline.

         • There is initially no prior basis under which the two poles are primitive separate things.
         • Therefore the poles may not be read as two externally separated primitive things.
         • Any admissible primitive origin must be read as one primitive condition bearing the opposition internally. This
           is a root discipline of the rewrite. It is not permission to smuggle in an unearned mediating substance.

When convenient, write Inv(x) for the following schematic situation:

         • x is an admissible primitive origin-condition,
         • x does not coincide with either isolated pole,
         • the non-identical sterile poles are opposed,
         • that opposition is internal to the one origin-condition,
         • and the condition does not terminate in pole-discharge. This shorthand is mnemonic. It does not replace proof
           burden.

2.6 Finite proof language for structural witnesses

The proofs above are stated in prose because the root vocabulary is intentionally prior to geometry and carrier machinery.
For the finite structural witnesses in Section 6, the same burden is written in a small Boolean language so the reader can
check the witness tables exactly.
For a candidate x, define the finite admissibility test
                   Adm(x) := NV(x) ∧ ¬Flat(x) ∧ BearOpp(x) ∧ OneOrigin(x) ∧ NoDischarge(x).
Here BearOpp(x) records that the primitive pole-opposition is borne internally, OneOrigin(x) records that the poles
have not been externalized into two prior primitive things, and NoDischarge(x) records that the branch has not collapsed
into either isolated terminal pole. In the witness tables, each predicate is evaluated as 0 or 1 and a candidate survives
exactly when Adm(x) = 1.
The same finite language is also used for the saturation and relation-grid witnesses:
                   S(n) = min(n, 9),       G2 = {0, 1, 2, 3} × {0, 1, 2, 3},     R2 = G2 \ {(0, 0)}.
The first expression is the declared same-axis decision witness used in Section 6.4: after index 9, a further genuinely
new burden is not counted as same-axis novelty. The second and third expressions define the smallest relation-grid
witness used in Section 6.5: the 2D grid has 16 placements and 15 nonzero relation vectors from the origin. These are
exact finite-cardinality checks, so the numerical tolerance is 0.

This is not a detachable extra premise about arbitrary worlds. It is the witness fixed by the subject matter of the

3.2 Nullity exclusion

Theorem 3.2.1 (Nullity exclusion).
Absolute nullity is not a viable realized root for a falsifiable law-bearing branch. Status: Proved in this CF. Proof. A
falsifiable law-bearing branch must support at least the distinction between admissible and inadmissible articulation
and between realized and unrealized burden. Absolute nullity supports no realized content and no realized distinction.
Therefore it cannot serve as the realized root of the present branch. □

3.3 Undifferentiated totality exclusion

Theorem 3.3.1 (Undifferentiated totality exclusion).
Absolute undifferentiated totality is not a viable realized root for a falsifiable law-bearing branch. Status: Proved in this
CF. Proof. A falsifiable law-bearing branch requires some realized noncoincidence by which one admissible condition
can fail to coincide with another or with itself in a relevant way. Absolute undifferentiated totality contains no internal
noncoincidence and therefore cannot sustain differentiated realized articulation. Hence it cannot serve as the realized
root of the present branch. □

3.4 Shared sterility does not collapse the poles

Proposition 3.4.1.
The poles 0 and 1 are not identical even though both are sterile for the present branch. Status: Proved in this CF. Proof.
By PB1, 0 ̸= 1 . By PB2, both are sterile. Distinctness and shared sterility therefore coexist. □

3.5 Exhaustive terminal classification at the primitive layer

Definition 3.5.1 (Terminal primitive pole-candidate).
A terminal primitive pole-candidate is a primitive origin-candidate considered only at terminal, flat, sterile resolution:
no internal noncoincidence is available by which differentiated articulation could be admitted, and no added basis is
available by which the candidate could be decomposed or separated.
Theorem 3.5.2 (No third terminal primitive pole survives).
Let t be any terminal primitive pole-candidate. Then exactly one of the following holds: 1. t = 0, 2. t = 1. In particular,
no third terminal primitive pole survives. Status: Proved in this CF. Proof. At the primitive layer, classification can
use only the weakest distinctions already on the page: whether any realized content is present at all, and whether any
internal noncoincidence is available.

       • If no realized content is present, the candidate is complete realized absence and so equals 0.
       • If realized content is present, the candidate is non-vacuous; but if it is also terminal, flat, and primitive, then
         no internal noncoincidence is available by which differentiated articulation could be admitted, and no richer
         content may be imported from outside. Therefore realized content is present yet internally undifferentiated,
         so the candidate collapses to 1. There is no third case. Any purported third primitive terminal pole either
         lacks realized content, possesses realized content without internal noncoincidence, or already contains richer
         articulated burden and therefore is not a terminal primitive pole-candidate. Hence no third terminal primitive
         pole survives. □

3.6 Forced admissible origin theorem

Theorem 3.6.1 (Forced admissible origin theorem).
For the realized branch under study, the primitive origin-condition ω is one primitive condition bearing the opposition
of 0 and 1 internally without terminal settlement. Equivalently,
Inv(ω). Status: Proved in this CF. Proof. By Scope Convention 3.1.2, ω is the primitive origin-condition of a realized
falsifiable branch. By Theorem 3.2.1, ω ̸= 0. By Theorem 3.3.1, ω ̸= 1. By Theorem 3.5.2, there is no third terminal
primitive pole. Therefore the realized branch-origin cannot be any terminally settled primitive pole at all. By Section
2.4, the primitive layer contains no prior basis under which two primitive poles could begin as externally separate things.
So the relevant primitive opposition cannot be realized as two already-distinct primitive objects. By Proposition 3.4.1,
the two poles are genuinely distinct even though both are sterile in isolation. Since the realized branch-origin cannot
terminate in either isolated pole, cannot be a third terminal primitive pole, and cannot externalize the poles into two
already-separated primitive things, the only admissible primitive possibility left is that the distinction between the two
terminal poles is borne internally by one primitive origin-condition without terminal settlement. That is exactly the
manuscript meaning of Inv(ω). □
Corollary 3.6.2 (No simpler primitive survivor remains).
Relative to the inferential discipline of this manuscript, no simpler primitive survivor remains beyond: 1. isolated nullity,
2. isolated undifferentiated totality, 3. one primitive origin-condition bearing their unresolved internal opposition. The
first two are excluded for any realized falsifiable branch, so only the third remains admissible. Status: Proved in this CF.

3.7 Invariant-bearing precludes flatness

Theorem 3.7.1 (Invariant-bearing is incompatible with flatness).

4.1 Dependence-ranked articulation chain

The articulation chain must now be read in dependence-ranked form: 1. one admissible origin-condition carries

question becomes whether that degree admits a genuinely higher-resolution differentiated degree under the same
invariant, 4. if such a degree is admitted, the lower degree is no longer sufficient as sole determination, 5. within an

This is not chronology. It is dependency order.

4.7 Same-axis saturation and forced orthogonal re-articulation

Definition 4.7.1 (Same-axis saturation).
The current admitted axis/domain is same-axis saturated when no genuinely new lawful invariant-bearing articulation
remains available within that same axis/domain. Any further purported articulation there would either be mere renaming
or reparameterization of what is already borne, or would require a new irreducible axis/domain and therefore not
belong to the same axis/domain. Clarification. Same-axis saturation is not discharge, not resolution of the invariant,
not collapse, not termination, and not a claim that nothing further happens. It is only exhaustion of lawful same-axis
expression.
Theorem 4.7.2 (Exhaustive bearing within an admitted axis/domain).
Within a currently admitted axis/domain, any genuinely new lawful same-axis invariant-bearing articulation belongs
to the articulation burden of that same axis/domain. It cannot be omitted and still count as lawful completion of
that axis/domain. Status: Proved in this CF. Proof. Let the current axis/domain be admitted. Suppose a genuinely
new lawful same-axis invariant-bearing articulation remains available there and yet is left outside the articulation
burden of that same axis/domain. Then the axis/domain is being treated as lawfully complete while lawful same-axis
invariant-bearing articulation still remains unborne there. That is not completion of the current axis/domain at all; it is
incomplete same-axis bearing falsely treated as closure. Since Theorem 4.3.1 makes non-bearing contradictory for
admitted articulation, lawful same-axis invariant-bearing articulation cannot be discarded as though it were irrelevant to
the current axis/domain. Therefore any genuinely new lawful same-axis invariant-bearing articulation belongs to the
articulation burden of that same axis/domain and cannot be omitted while claiming lawful completion there. □
Theorem 4.7.3 (Saturation theorem).
Within a given admitted orthogonality class, once lawful same-axis expression of the invariant is exhaustively saturated,
further admitted articulation in that same class is impossible without contradiction. Since terminal discharge is excluded,
orthogonal re-articulation is forced. Status: Proved in this CF.
Proof. By Definition 4.7.1, same-axis saturation means no genuinely new lawful invariant-bearing articulation remains
available within the current axis/domain. Suppose nevertheless that a further admitted articulation exists in that same
class. If it is genuinely new and lawful, then Definition 4.7.1 was false and the axis/domain was not saturated after all. If
it is not genuinely new, then it is only renaming or reparameterization of what is already borne and therefore not a new
admitted articulation in that class. Contradiction. Therefore no further admitted articulation exists there once same-axis
saturation holds. But if admitted articulation ceased altogether, the invariant would have no borne expression and would
be discharged, contradicting Theorem 4.3.1. Hence further articulation must occur, but not in the saturated class. By
Theorem 4.6.1, it is forced to occur as orthogonal re-articulation. □
